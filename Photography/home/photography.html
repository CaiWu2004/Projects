Nom Wah Tea Parlor:

Was built in 1920 in Doyer St during a time where chinese immigrants were being isolated and banned from entering the workforce. During this time, Nom Wah Tea Parlor was an icon for Chinese culture, specifically authentic Chinese food. Allowing Chinese immigrants to get that familiar taste of food from China, it was also a place that spread Chinese cuisine to America. After surviving through multiple disasters in America, they still stay standing to this day, right at Doyer St, even if the ownership has changed hands.

Mcdonald in Doyers:

During the construction of Chinatown, all the buildings used were old tenement buildings left behind by immigrants like the Italians, Christian Germans, Jews, Irish, Scots, as well as somenative-born Americans and African Americans. It was after these immigrants moved out and Chinese immigrants started moving in that they occupied these old tenements. For one of these, in the 1900s, the owner of this specific tenement decided to add green tiles and pagoda-style decoration to make it look more Chinese. This is a similar case for most tenement buildings in Chinatown, where the residents add their own decorations, making it into a place that blends American and Chinese culture. During the 1980s-1990s, McDonald's moved into one of these redecorated tenements and kept the pagoda design. As a result, it became one of, if not the only american fast food restaurants that combines Chinese culture with an American industry. They even had a native Chinese speaker and Chinese menus for locals, as well as catering to Americans. It was only later on, when rent prices increased, that McDonald's moved or went out of business.

Canel Arcade:

During its construction, Canal Street was built over a drain/sewer system in the early 1800s, which made its condition terrible and officially made it one of the earliest slum areas. As a result, to make money and survive, stores sell cheap accessories, entertainment, saloons, and loading houses. Later on, the Canel Arcade was built, which was a long tunnel with shops and stalls selling cheap goods and markets. As chinatown expended later on, this place became a business area for Chinese immigrants, specifically Chinese restaurants and stores. I remember specifically, from childhood to highschoool I have visited this place multiple times, specifically for catering needs. However, it was during my high school years that the place shut down and went under construction to this day. From the closure of this commercial area, it can be seen that Canel Street is mainly transitioning from small businesses to upscale restaurants and tourism attraction as evidenced by larger shops opening nearby. However, even though this place was one of the biggest representations of Chinatowns' growth and change throughout the decades.
