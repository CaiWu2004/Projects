Nom Wah Tea Parlor:

Built in 1920 in Doyer St during a time where chinese immigrants were being isolated and banned from entering the workforce. During this time, Nom Wah Tea Parlor was an icon for Chinese culture, specifically authentic Chinese food. Allowing Chinese immigrants to get that familiar taste of food from China, it was also a place that spread Chinese cuisine to America. After surviving through multiple disasters in America, they still stay standing to this day right at Doyer St, even if the ownership has changed hands.

Mcdonald in Doyers:

The McDonald’s near Doyers Street in Manhattan’s Chinatown stood out because it was inside a building that looked like a traditional Chinese temple, with a pagoda-style roof and red columns. The building itself wasn’t originally a McDonald’s—it was part of Chinatown’s older structures that were later redesigned to reflect Chinese culture and attract visitors. When McDonald’s moved in, it kept this design, creating a unique mix of American fast food and Chinese architectural style. It served both locals and tourists, often using bilingual signage. Although it eventually closed due to changes like rising rents, it remains a memorable example of how cultures can blend in a place like Chinatown.

Canel Arcade:

The Canal Arcade near Bowery and Canal Street reflects a period when Chinatown and the Bowery were defined by dense, small-scale commerce. These indoor corridors of shops allowed immigrant entrepreneurs to operate in tight urban spaces, contributing to Canal Street’s identity as a hub for affordable goods and informal retail. Today, the gated and abandoned structure represents the decline of this retail model, highlighting broader changes in the neighborhood driven by rising rents and modernization.
