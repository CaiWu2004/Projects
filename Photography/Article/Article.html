When it comes to New York City, one of the most popular main attractions has to be Manhattan Chinatown, the oldest in the city by far. Chinatown can be dated back to the mid-1800s, even before it was built. During the California Gold Rush in the 1840s–1850s and the railroad construction, many Chinese immigrants moved to New York City to escape violence and discrimination. Chinese immigrants weren’t kindly received by Americans due to how cheap their labor cost was, leading many jobs to hire them for cheap labor over Americans. In addition, with the construction of railroads, Chinese immigrants were given the most dangerous tasks, planting and lighting bombs for tunneling.
To escape these conditions, many Chinese immigrants moved east, away from California, and many took root in New York City. Specifically, they resided within a small area of Manhattan, being Mott Street, Pell Street, and Doyers Street, making it the basic prototype of present-day New York City.
As Chinese immigrants moved in, the construction of Chinatown, a place they could call home, began to be built. However, unlike some Chinatowns in San Francisco, Manhattan Chinatown was not built from scratch; it was built using the old residential buildings left over from the Irish, Jewish, and Italian immigrant communities as a foundation, evident from the fact that Chinatown borders Little Italy, the Italian community of Manhattan. While the old residential buildings were used as a foundation for present-day Chinatown, the condition of the buildings wasn’t the best; it was actually quite bad. The apartments were small, the hallways were narrow, and they were not well-maintained. However, being new to the area, Chinese immigrants had to make do with what they had; by that, I meant 5–15 people per room. It wasn’t the best of conditions. However, it was through this living environment that they slowly took these old, cramped, and broken apartments as a foundation and built their own homes around them.
However, during the shaping of Chinatowns, one of the main factors contributing to their development was the Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882, which prevented more Chinese from immigrating to America, as well as discrimination against Chinese immigrants, preventing them from being legally hired in the workforce. These two factors caused the Chinese community in New York to be more male-dominant due to the low female-to-male ratio; as such, Chinese immigrants built their own system. Political associations, underground businesses, drug trade, etc. They relied on themselves to build a place for themselves within a city and country that didn’t welcome them.
During this period of isolation and exclusion from the Americans, education became an important aspect of community development as the Chinese immigrants had to rely on themselves within this new land. As such, schools near that taught Chinese immigrants and their children how to read, write, and speak english was establiosh. One of them was the one between Mott Street and Elizabeth Street, which was exhausted since the 2000s when my parents first immigrated to America. Unfortunately, the school has seize toe xiosts and has since shut down.
As a result, in this time period, Chinatown, specifically near Doyers Street, or the “Bloody Angle,” was one of the places with the highest crime rates. Opium dens, prostitution, societies, gangs, wars between groups, gun shootouts—the Doyers Street corner was known for being the most dangerous place, with conflicts between different Chinese gangs.
However, while it was known for its danger, Doyers Street was also known for its Chinese cuisine. Due to the Chinese Exclusion Act, as well as being isolated, Chinese immigrants needed a way to get the familiar taste of China. As such, many tea houses and rice shops appeared one after another. One of them specifically was Nom Wah Tea Parlor on Doyers Street, a restaurant that sells tea and dim sum, as well as being a bakery mainly known for its mooncakes. During this time, it became one of the most iconic sources of Chinese food for Chinese immigrants who had been away from their home country and couldn’t return—the tea and the dim sum from their home country. While Nom Wah Tea Parlor became an iconic part of Doyers Street and for the Chinese community, it has not been doing well since the late 1900s to the present day. The ownership of the parlor changed hands once, and with new bakeries and restaurants popping up in recent years, Nom Wah Tea Parlor is facing a steady decline, especially since the owner, in his 80s, no longer has any desire to keep improving the place.
Additionally, during this time, Doyers Street, being a slum area, was a major tourist attraction for wealthy uptowners. For the wealthy, it was an interesting sight to behold as they toured around Chinatown, looking at the sights of poverty. It was also during this time that the first Chinese American fusion dish was created, using Chinese techniques and style while also catering to Americans. It was called chop suey, a “flexible, thrown-together stir-fry” (Sietsema), with chow mein featuring crunchy noodles as its close follower (Sietsema), as well as “egg foo young: a wok-fried omelet full of Chinese vegetables, and sometimes meat or seafood, smothered in European-style brown gravy, and placed on top of fluffy and fragrant polished rice” (Sietsema). Later on, Chinese American cuisine would continue to improve and change, but it cannot be denied that the first fusion was in Chinatown.
Finally, in 1943, when the Chinese Exclusion Act was lifted, a surge of Chinese immigrants, mainly from the mainland, came into America, and Manhattan Chinatown was one of the communities that received this surge of immigrants. Since then, present-day Chinatown is ten times the size it was at the beginning.
Today, Chinatown continues to change as new businesses and restaurants replace older ones. While historical establishments like Nom Wah Tea Parlor remain important icons for Chinese immigrants and culture in New York City, many such establishments have closed due to rising rent and changing demographics. Even so, new and more modern restaurants and businesses have opened in their place, showing that while Chinatown has moved toward modernization, the influence of older businesses still remains today.
