When it comes to New York City, one of the most popular main attractions has to be the Manhattan Chinatown, the oldest in New York City by far. China Town can be dated back to the mid-1800s, even before it was built. During the California Gold Rush in the 1840s - 1850s and the railroad construction, many Chinese immigrants moved to New York City to escape the violence and discrimination. Chinese immigrants weren’t kindly taken by the Americans due to how cheap their labor cost, leading many jobs to hire them for cheap labor over Americans. In addition, with the construction of railroads, Chinese immigrants were given the most dangerous task, planting and lighting the bombs for tunneling. To escape these conditions, many Chinese immigrants moved east, away from Californai and many took root in New York City. Specifically, they resided within a small area of Manhattan, being Mott Street, Pell Street, and Doyer Street, making it the basic prototype of present-day New York City. As Chinese immigrants moved in, the construction of Chinatown, a place they could call home, began to be built. However, unlike some Chinatowns in Sans francisco, the Manhattan Chinatown was not built from scratch; it was built using the old residential buildings left over from the Irish, Jewish, and Italian immigrant community as a foundation, evident from the fact that Chinatown borders Little Italy, the Italian community of Manhattan. While the old residential buildings were used as a foundation for present day ChinaTown, the condition of the buildings wasn’t the best; it was actually quite bad. The apartments were small, the hallways were narrow, and it wasn’t maintained well. However, with being new to the area, the Chinese immigrants had to amke due with what they had, by that I meant 5-15 people per room. It wasn’t the best of conditions. However, it was through this living environment that they slowly took these old, cramped, and broken apartments as a foundation and built their own homes around them. However, during the shaping of Chinatowns, one of the main factors contributing to the development of Chinatowns was the Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882, which prevented more Chinese from immigrating to America, as well as discrimination against the Chinese immigrants, preventing them from getting legally hired in the workforce. These two factors caused the Chinese community in New York to be more male-dominant due to the low female-to-male ratio; as such, the Chinese immigrants built their own system. Political associations, underground businesses, drug trade, etc. They relied on themselves to build a place for themselves within a city and country that doesn’t welcome them. Finally, in 1943, when the Chinese Exclusion Act was lifted, a surge of Chinese immigrants, mainly from the mainland, surged into America, and Manhattan Chinatown was one of the communities that received the surge of immigrating Chinese. With the new group of immigrants coming in, Chinatown saw a growth in itself. It started expanding outwards, building residential areas, a garment business, a laundry business, and mostly restaurants.
